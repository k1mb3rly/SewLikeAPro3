Part 2: Tracing & Sewing Darts

How to transfer dart lines onto your interfaced fabric, and a step-by-step guide to sewing and pressing fish-eye darts.

Tracing Darts onto Fabric

Important Order

Interface the fabric first, then trace darts. Do not trace onto uninterfaced fabric and then fuse — the heat and pressure can distort your markings.

What You Need

  • Dressmaker's tracing paper
  • Tracing wheel
  • Paper pattern with dart markings

What to Trace

  • Both dart leg lines (the two triangles of a fish-eye dart)
  • The horizontal waist line (where the two triangles meet)

Buttonhole Markings

Trace buttonhole positions onto the paper pattern only — not onto the fabric yet. You'll transfer these later when you're closer to that construction step.

Sewing Fish-Eye Darts

A fish-eye dart is diamond-shaped — two triangles meeting at the waist. It creates waist shaping on both the front and back of the jacket.

Step-by-Step

  1. Fold fabric right sides together along the dart center line.
  2. Sew from one point to the waist mark, stopping at the waist clip mark.
  3. Sew from the other point to the waist mark from the opposite direction.
  4. Do NOT backstitch at the points. Instead, leave long thread tails and tie off with hand-knotted knots.
  5. Clip at the waist mark (the horizontal line) to allow the two halves to be pressed in opposite directions.
  6. Press the upper half toward the side seam. Press the lower half toward the side seam.

Pressing

  • Press both halves toward the side seam.
  • Use a place-and-lift iron technique — do not slide the iron along the dart, as this can stretch the bias.

Why No Backstitching?

Backstitching at dart points creates a visible lump. Hand-knotting the tails gives a smooth, tapered end without bulk.
