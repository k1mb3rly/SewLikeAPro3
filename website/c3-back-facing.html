Part 2: Mandatory Edit — Creating the Back Neck Facing

Everyone does this edit. It restores the full facing family and makes collar attachment significantly easier.

Why This Edit Is Required

The original pattern has no back neck facing — the back lining extends all the way up to the collar. This makes construction harder (you lose half the facing family from the Class 2 practice). Creating a separate back neck facing restores the full facing family and makes collar attachment significantly easier.

Step A: Create the Back Neck Facing Pattern

You will use the back LINING pattern (not main) as your starting template.

  1. Place a piece of pattern paper over just the top area of the back lining pattern.
  2. Trace the neckline curve.
  3. Trace 2¾″ down the center back from the neckline. Make a mark.
  4. Trace 2¾″ down the shoulder from the neckline. Make a mark.
  5. Connect the two marks with a smooth scoop curve. Use your ruler set at 2¾″: bring it to the neckline and keep rotating it along the curve, tracing the bottom edge as you go. Then smooth the curve with a French curve ruler.
  6. Cut out this piece. This is your back neck facing — but it still needs one more step.
  7. Add ½″ seam allowance to the bottom curved edge ONLY. The shoulder, center back, and neckline edges already have seam allowance from the original pattern. Only the new scooped edge needs it.

Why Only the Bottom Edge Gets Seam Allowance

EdgeSeam Allowance?
Shoulder edgeAlready included (from original pattern)
Center back edgeAlready included (from original pattern)
Neckline curveAlready included (from original pattern)
Bottom scoopNEW — needs ½″ seam allowance added

Rule: Any time you chop a pattern, the chopped edge loses its seam allowance and needs a new one.

Step B: Modify the Back Lining Pattern

Now you need to remove the facing area from the back lining so you don't have double fabric at the neck.

  1. On your traced back lining pattern, measure 2¾″ down from the neckline on both the center back and the shoulder, just as you did above.
  2. Draw the same scoop curve connecting those points.
  3. Remove the upper section (this is what became your facing).
  4. Add ½″ seam allowance to the NEW top edge of the back lining (the scooped edge where you just cut). This is the opposite direction from the facing — the lining needs seam allowance going up, the facing needs it going down.

How These Connect

When assembled, the back neck facing (cut in shell fabric) gets sewn to the top of the back lining at this curved seam. Together they replace the original single back lining piece.

The facing gives you structure at the neckline and restores the full facing family for easier collar construction.

Fabric Note

The back neck facing is cut from your main (shell) fabric, not lining.
