Part 3: Bicep Check & Optional Pattern Edits

Required bicep measurement check plus three optional fit adjustments.

Bicep Measurement Check (Required)

This is not optional. Before cutting, measure the circumference of your bicep and compare it to the finished bicep measurement for your size. The measurements on the board represent the finished jacket measurement at the bicep.

Bicep Fit Rule

Your arm measurement should be at LEAST ½″ smaller than the finished jacket bicep. If your bicep is the same size as or larger than the chart number, the sleeve will be too tight.

How to Measure

Wrap a measuring tape around the fullest part of your upper arm (bicep). Don't flex. Compare this number to the chart for your size.

Need an Adjustment?

If you need a bicep adjustment, the instructor will help you extend the sleeve pattern individually. Do NOT just trace a larger size for the sleeve — different sizes won't fit together at the armhole.

Optional Pattern Edits

These three edits are entirely optional. Do all, some, or none based on your fit preferences.

Edit A: Changing the Jacket Body Length

The jacket length is measured from high point shoulder (where your shoulder meets your neck, on top) straight down the front of the body.

How to Check

  1. Find the front main pattern.
  2. Note the small notch/cutout at the bottom hem — this is where the lining connects. The actual hem length is at the shorter outer edge, not the extended lining-connection side.
  3. Measure from high point shoulder straight down to that hem edge. (Example: 1X = 36″.)
  4. Hold your tape measure at your own high point shoulder, let it hang over your chest, and see where that length falls on your body.

How to Shorten

Do Not Simply Trim the Bottom

You'll lose the hem shaping. Instead:

  1. Trace the full front pattern onto paper.
  2. Determine how many inches to remove.
  3. Draw a horizontal line across the pattern in the lower third area (below the waist, above the hem shaping).
  4. Slice the paper at that line. Overlap the two pieces by your shortening amount. Tape together.

This preserves the bottom hem shape while removing length from the middle.

IMPORTANT

If you change the body length, you MUST make the same change to ALL FOUR body pieces: front main, back main, front lining, and back lining. All by the same amount.

Edit B: Changing the Sleeve Length

This jacket has a two-piece sleeve (upper and lower). The pattern includes a lengthen/shorten line marked across both sleeve pieces.

How to Check

  1. Place your tape measure at the top of your shoulder (where the armhole seam would sit).
  2. Run the tape down the outside of your arm to your desired sleeve length.
  3. On the upper sleeve pattern, measure from the top center down to the actual hem. The hem is where the small notch/cutout ends and the edge becomes a straight line. (Example: 1X = 25″.)
  4. Compare your arm measurement to the pattern measurement.

How to Adjust

  • Find the lengthen/shorten line printed on the pattern (it runs horizontally across the sleeve).
  • To lengthen: Slice at the line. Spread the pieces apart by your desired amount. Tape pattern paper behind the gap.
  • To shorten: Slice at the line. Overlap the pieces by your desired amount. Tape together.

IMPORTANT

Apply the same adjustment to both the upper and lower sleeve for both main and lining patterns.

Edit C: Adding Fish-Eye Darts for Waist Shaping

The jacket is designed as a straight, unisex cut with no darts. If you want more of an hourglass silhouette, you can add fish-eye darts to all four body pieces.

IMPORTANT

If you add darts, you MUST add them to all four body pieces (front main, back main, front lining, back lining). Doing only fronts and not backs will throw off the garment's balance and the side seams will swing. It's all or nothing.

Sizing Note for Darts

  • If you're between sizes AND adding darts: trace the LARGER size (the darts will take in the extra).
  • If you're between sizes and NOT adding darts: trace the smaller size.
  • Do not size down AND add darts — you'll over-reduce the ease and the jacket may not close.

Measurements You Need

  • High point shoulder (HPS) to apex: measure from the top of your shoulder down to the fullest point of your chest.
  • High point shoulder to natural waist: let the tape hang down your front and note where your natural waist falls.
  • Apex to apex: measure across your chest between the two fullest points. Divide this number in half.

Placing the Front Dart (Main Fabric)

  1. On the front main pattern, find the button marking. The button line represents center front (NOT the cut edge of the pattern, which extends beyond center for the overlap).
  2. From the button, measure inward toward the side seam by your half apex-to-apex measurement. Mark this point — this is your dart's horizontal position.
  3. From HPS, measure down to your apex measurement. Mark it. Then go 1″ below that mark — this is the top vanishing point of the dart.
  4. From HPS, measure down to your waist measurement. Mark this line — this is the widest point of the dart.
  5. From the waist mark, measure 4″ further down. Mark this — this is the bottom vanishing point of the dart.
  6. At the waist mark, measure ½″ on each side of your dart line (1″ total dart width). Mark both points.
  7. Using a ruler, connect the top vanishing point to each side of the waist mark, then connect each side of the waist mark down to the bottom vanishing point. You should have a diamond/fish-eye shape.

Placing the Back Dart (Main Fabric)

  1. Measure the width of the back pattern. Find the center.
  2. Place the dart at center, using the same waist and length measurements as the front.
  3. Same 1″ width, same fish-eye shape.

Placing the Back Lining Dart

Same as the back main dart — center of the pattern, identical measurements.

Placing the Front Lining Dart (Different Method)

The front lining does not extend to center front — the facing covers that area. If you placed a dart in the middle of the lining, it wouldn't align correctly when the lining is sewn to the facing. Instead, the dart is absorbed into the facing seam.

  1. On the front lining pattern, find the long curved edge that gets sewn to the facing.
  2. Use the same vertical measurements (apex minus 1″ for top, waist for middle, waist plus 4″ for bottom) but place them along that curved seam line instead of in the center of the pattern.
  3. At the waist point on the seam, measure 1″ inward (toward the side seam). Mark it.
  4. Draw smooth curves (not straight lines) from the top vanishing point to the 1″ inward mark, and from the 1″ mark back out to the bottom vanishing point. The seam line should curve in and out like a gentle hourglass. Use a French curve for smoothness — no points or angles.

Why Smooth Curves Matter

The front lining dart must feel smooth, not pointed. If you draw straight lines to the inward point, you'll get a visible angle at the waist when the lining is sewn to the facing. Curved lines blend the shaping gradually and look professional.
