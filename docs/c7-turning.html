Parts 5 & 6: Turning & Sleeve Lining

Turning the jacket right side out through the lining turning hole, handling the hem overhang, and attaching each sleeve lining using the reach-through technique.

Part 5: Turning the Jacket Right Side Out

This is where the turning hole in the lining center back (from Class 5) finally gets used.

Turning the Jacket

  1. Reach through the turning hole in the lining center back.
  2. Grab sections of the jacket and pull them through the hole. Start with the collar — it's the farthest away and the most structured.
  3. Work down from the collar. When you reach the shoulder pads, isolate each one and maneuver it through individually.
  4. Pull the sleeves through one at a time.

Take Your Time

The hole is small by design — it needs to be hand-sewn closed later. Pulling too aggressively can stress the stitching around the hole.

The Hem Overhang

Once the jacket is right side out, find the front facing corners at the bottom. The bubble you left earlier now tucks inward:

  1. Using your thumb, push the bubble fabric inward so the hem lies flat.
  2. The result is a slight overhang where the jacket wool extends past the lining at the hem edge. This is intentional and correct.
  3. Press this edge flat. The overhang means you see wool at the edge of your jacket, not lining peeking out.

The small tucked area at each front corner can be slip-stitched closed by hand later.

Same Principle at the Sleeve Hem

The jacket sleeve is longer than the lining sleeve. When finished, the wool folds over at the bottom of each sleeve, creating the same clean overhang. You see wool at the cuff edge, not lining.

This is why the 2″ interfacing strip went at the bottom of the sleeve — it gives you a crisp, structured fold at that edge.

Part 6: Attaching the Sleeve Lining

Each sleeve lining gets sewn to its jacket sleeve at the hem opening. This is done one sleeve at a time using the turning hole.

Positioning the Sleeve Lining

Take the lining sleeve and push it down into the jacket sleeve, as if you're putting your arm into the jacket.

Check Seam Alignment

Make sure the seams of the lining sleeve align with the seams of the jacket sleeve (upper seam to upper seam, lower seam to lower seam). If the seams don't align, the sleeve is twisted — pull it out and reposition.

The Reach-Through Technique

  1. Reach your hand through the turning hole in the lining center back.
  2. Your arm goes between the lining and the jacket, up into the sleeve.
  3. Your hand comes out at the bottom of the sleeve, between the lining layer and the jacket layer.
  4. Grab the raw edge of the jacket sleeve hem and fold it inward.
  5. Grab the raw edge of the lining sleeve hem and fold it inward.
  6. Pinch both folded edges together with your hand.
  7. Without letting go, pull your arm back out through the turning hole, bringing the pinched edges with you.
  8. You now have both raw edges, right sides together, pulled out through the hole.

Pinning & Sewing the Sleeve Hem

  1. Pin at both sleeve seam intersections first (line up lining seam allowance to jacket seam allowance).
  2. Work around the circle, pinning right sides together, raw edge to raw edge.
  3. It will look like the two sleeve layers are stuck together in a funky loop. That's correct.
  4. Sew at ½″ seam allowance around the full circle. Stop and reposition every inch or two — it's a tight circle with a lot of fabric.
  5. Backstitch at start and end. Clip threads.

Push Back Through & Press

  1. Reach in from the front of the sleeve and pull the lining back through so the sleeve is right side out again.
  2. The jacket sleeve will overhang the lining sleeve at the bottom, creating the same clean wool-edge finish as the body hem.
  3. Press the sleeve hem edge crisp and flat.
  4. Repeat for the second sleeve.
