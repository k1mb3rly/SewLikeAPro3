Parts 4–6: Grading, Joining & Clipping

Grading seam allowances to reduce bulk, pinning and sewing the full perimeter, and all the clips before turning.

Part 4: Grading Seam Allowances

Grading means trimming one seam allowance shorter than the other to reduce bulk where two interfaced layers meet. This is done after pressing the seam open.

Where to Grade

Grade the collar seam (where the collar attaches to the neckline) on both families. If you also interfaced your facings, grade those shoulder seams too — anywhere two interfaced layers meet.

Press Open Before Grading

Press the seams open before grading. It's much easier to press when both seam allowances are still full width. Trying to press a tiny graded seam allowance flat is harder and you'll wish you'd done it in the right order.

How to Grade

  1. Press the seam open first (this separates the allowances and makes them easier to cut).
  2. Trim one of the two seam allowances to half its current width. Leave the other at full width.
  3. Be careful to only cut the seam allowance — not the garment fabric.

Part 5: Joining the Two Families

Once both families are assembled, pressed, and graded, put them right sides together and sew around the entire perimeter: up the front, around the collar, down the other front.

Pinning Order

  1. Lay the jacket and facing families right sides together.
  2. Match and pin the collar points first (both sides).
  3. Shake out and find the middle of the collar curve. Pin.
  4. Make sure all seam allowances are pressed open at every point you cross.
  5. Work toward the collar's 90° notch corners. Pin those precisely.
  6. Match and pin the break notches on both sides.
  7. Pin the bottom corners of each front.
  8. Pin between all major points (middle, middle, middle).

Sewing Path

You are not sewing from the raw bottom edge all the way. On each front, start sewing approximately ½″ in from the raw edge of the hem extension — where the extension meets the jacket body. Leave a small flat area unsewn at the very bottom. This gap is needed later when the lining connects.

  1. Start: ½″ in from the raw hem edge. Backstitch.
  2. Sew up the front of the jacket.
  3. Pivot at the corner where the front meets the collar notch (stop, needle down, pivot).
  4. Come onto the collar before pivoting at the 90° notch corner.
  5. Sew up to the collar point. Pivot.
  6. Sew around the curved top of the collar.
  7. Pivot at the other collar point.
  8. Come onto the jacket before pivoting off the collar.
  9. Sew down the other front.
  10. Stop: ½″ in from the raw hem edge on the other side. Backstitch.

The 90° Notch Corner Rule

At the 90° notch corners: you must sew onto the collar before pivoting (just like in the muslin practice). If you pivot at the edge, you'll miss the collar. Same going the other direction — come onto the jacket before pivoting off the collar.

Part 6: Clipping

After sewing, clip before turning. There are more clips on this jacket than the muslin practice.

Clip Locations

  • 6 diagonal corner clips: bottom front corner (×2), collar points where it meets the notch (×2), and the top collar points (×2). Clip diagonally across each corner.
  • Collar curve: multiple small clips around the curved top of the collar, more densely at the curvier edges near the corners.
  • 90° notch corners (×2): clip all the way into the corner without hitting the 90° stitching. You can clip through other stitch lines that cross the path — just not the actual corner stitch. The corner must break open into a completely straight line. If it doesn't open fully, the corner will be bumpy when turned.
  • Break notch clips (×2): check that the clips at the break go as close to the stitch line as possible without cutting the stitching. If you were conservative earlier, deepen them now. These clips must allow you to change pressing direction at the break.

Clean Out the Bulk

After clipping, use a point turner to clean all seam allowance bulk out of every corner. This is especially important where interfacing adds thickness. When turning corners with a point turner, push the fabric — don't jab the tool or you'll poke through.
