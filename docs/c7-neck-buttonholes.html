Parts 1 & 2: Neck Seam Tack & Buttonhole Marking

Tacking the collar neck seam allowances together before attaching the lining, then thread-tracing buttonhole positions onto the right side of the jacket.

Part 1: Tacking the Neck Seam Allowances Together

Before attaching the lining, you need to tack the two collar neckline seam allowances together so the collars don't flop apart once the lining is attached. This is the same technique used on the Sew Like a Pro 2 jacket.

What You're Joining

You have two neck seams:

  • Where the under collar attaches to the jacket neckline
  • Where the top collar attaches to the facing neckline

Both seam allowances should already be pressed open from last week. You're tacking seam allowance to seam allowance — not through the visible garment fabric.

How to Tack

  1. Thread a hand-sewing needle with doubled thread. Knot the end.
  2. Reach between the collar layers and grab the seam allowance from the under collar neck seam and the seam allowance from the top collar neck seam.
  3. Both seams should be pressed open, so you're only sewing through two layers of fabric (one seam allowance from each side), not four.
  4. Using a running stitch (in, out, in, out), sew the two seam allowances together from one shoulder to the other shoulder — just the width of the back neck.
  5. Knot off at the other shoulder.

Stitch Length

Don't overthink the stitch length. This is a functional tack, not decorative. Just work your way across at a comfortable pace.

Part 2: Marking Buttonholes with Thread Tracing

You're marking the buttonhole position on the right side of your jacket using thread — not chalk or markers, since those may not come out of wool.

Thread Only — No Chalk or Markers

Chalk and fabric markers may not come out of wool. Use thread tracing for all markings on your finished jacket.

Getting Buttonholes Sewn — Two Options

Option A: Home Machine

Use your home machine's buttonhole foot/function.

Test First

Take two scraps of your wool (to simulate the doubled front) plus a scrap of interfacing. Sew a test buttonhole through all layers before touching your jacket. Most buttonhole feet have a slider where you insert your button — the machine sizes the hole to fit.

Option B: Professional Service

Bring your marked jacket to a buttonhole professional — they'll ask: rectangle, keyhole, or rounded oval? Your choice. They sew to your thread markings.

  • Steinlauf & Stoller (Garment District) — Recommended. Not open weekends.
  • Jonathan's (Garment District)

Option C: Hand-Sewn Buttonholes

If you'd like to hand-sew your buttonholes, that's also an option. They'll look beautiful. It's just more time-intensive.

How to Mark with Thread

  1. Lay your jacket front flat, right side up.
  2. Place your paper pattern on top, aligned. Remember: the seam allowance is now tucked inside, so the pattern will extend slightly past the jacket edges. Align carefully.
  3. Thread a needle with a single thread (not doubled). No knot.
  4. At one end of the buttonhole marking: sew through the pattern and fabric, then back up. Leave thread tails hanging on both sides. Cut the thread, leaving the tails.
  5. Repeat at the other end of the buttonhole marking.
  6. Remove the pattern. You now have two sets of small thread tails marking both ends of the buttonhole.

If you're doing this at home: the thread marks tell you where to position your buttonhole foot. If you're having it done professionally, both marks are critical — they define the exact length and position of the hole.

Multiple Buttons

If you want multiple buttons, mark each one along the center front line. Typically they're evenly spaced, but spacing is a design choice.
